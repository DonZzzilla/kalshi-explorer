💡 What is Kalshi? TEENAGER

You bet money on yes or no. Right answer = win money. Wrong answer = lose money. That's it.

Kalshi is a website where grown-ups bet on whether things will happen. Like "will it rain tomorrow?" You pick YES or NO. If you're right, you win money. If you're wrong, you lose what you bet.

Kalshi is a prediction market where you trade on the outcome of real-world events. Each market has a YES and NO price in cents (1-99). If you buy YES at 65¢ and the event happens, you get $1.00 per contract. If not, you lose your 65¢.

Kalshi is a CFTC-regulated Designated Contract Market (DCM) and the first federally licensed exchange for event contracts in the US. Each contract is a binary option: pays $1.00 if the specified event resolves YES, $0.00 if NO. Prices are quoted in cents (1-99) representing the market's implied probability of YES — a 65¢ YES implies a 65% chance the market-makers collectively assign. Spreads, depth, and order books are visible via the API at /v2/markets/{ticker}/orderbook.

Example: "Dow Above 45000" — YES price is 12¢
This means the market thinks there's a 12% chance the Dow closes above 45000.
Buy 100 YES contracts at 12¢ each = $12 invested. If Dow > 45000, you get $100. Profit = $88.

📊 TOP 10 BY EDGE what is edge? Edge How sure everyone is. Big number = very sure. How confident the market is. Big gap = very sure. Small gap = could go either way. The difference between YES and NO prices. A 90¢ YES / 10¢ NO market has an edge of 80¢ — meaning the market is very confident. Small edges (like 5¢) mean the outcome is uncertain. High edge = high confidence, but lower profit potential per trade. Edge = |YES_price − NO_price| in cents. A 50/50 market has 0¢ edge; a near-certain market approaches 100¢. Edge is inversely related to expected profit per contract — high-edge markets pay out less on a win because the YES price is already high. The most profitable trades are usually in the 60-80¢ edge band.

💡 Earnings: What Could I Make? TEENAGER

The picture shows how much money you win at different prices. Cheap ticket = win less. Expensive ticket = win more but lose more often.

This picture shows how much money you could win or lose at different prices. Cheap tickets win less money. Expensive tickets win more money, but you lose more often.

Imagine you find a market where the YES price is 70¢. That means you pay 70¢ per contract. If you're right, your contract pays $1.00. If you're wrong, you get nothing.

The earnings function is a piecewise linear payoff: profit_per_contract = (100 − price) cents on a win, −price cents on a loss. Expected value per contract is p × (100−p) − (1−p) × p = 0 under the null hypothesis that the market is correctly priced. To produce positive EV you need p̂ > p — i.e. your estimated probability must exceed the implied price. The breakeven win-rate at any price p is p / 100 (so 70¢ requires ≥70% accuracy to break even before fees).

Think of it like this:
• Price = what you pay for a ticket
• $1.00 = what the ticket pays if you win
• Profit = $1.00 minus what you paid
So at 70¢, every winning ticket makes 30¢ profit. But you lose your 70¢ on every losing ticket.

This page connects to Kalshi's API, grabs market data, crunches the numbers, and displays them. Click any box in the pipeline below to learn what it does — explained simply.

The architecture is a five-stage data pipeline. Stage 1 hits Kalshi's public REST API (CORS forces a proxy in the browser-only deployment; a server-side bot can call directly). Stage 2 normalizes the response: tickers, prices, volume, and status. Stage 3 computes derived metrics — edge, mid-price, volume-weighted rank, and time-since-open. Stage 4 sorts and filters per the user's query. Stage 5 renders the views: market list, top-10 table, detail panel, and chart. Each stage is stateless and pure (modulo caching), so the same pipeline can be re-driven by a cron loop, a Discord slash command, or a Telegram bot with no rewrites.

Five-step REST round-trip: (1) fetch() with a query string to /v2/markets; (2) JSON parse into an array of market objects; (3) transform each object into a flat row of derived fields; (4) stable sort by a composite key (edge desc, volume desc); (5) DOM append into the result list. Network failures degrade to a cached or demo dataset; rate-limit responses (HTTP 429) trigger exponential backoff in the server-side variant.

⚖ The Prediction Market Ecology TEENAGER

⚠️ Based on research from the video "The Dark Truth About Prediction Markets" — a deep-dive into who actually makes money on Kalshi and Polymarket.

Most people lose money on prediction markets. A few smart people win most of the money. Big companies put in money so betting works. Some people cheat by knowing things before others. If you're going to play, you need to know which group you are.

Imagine a big game where most players lose their tokens. A tiny group of really smart players wins almost everything. And some players have secret cheat codes — they know what's going to happen before everyone else. That's what prediction markets are really like. This page explains who wins, who loses, and why.

Research shows that 71% of Polymarket users lose money, and only 28% make any profit. But it's even more extreme than that — the top 1% of traders make 95% of all profits, and the top 0.1% make 74%. This page breaks down the three groups who actually make money, and why the average person is funding their wins.

Academic studies of Polymarket activity (Nov 2022 – Sep 2025) reveal a power-law distribution of profits: ~71% of users are net losers, ~28% are net winners, but the profit concentration is extreme — top 1% capture 95% of profits, top 0.1% capture 74%. The profit/loss chart is logarithmic: each step on the Y-axis is 10×. On a linear scale the green winners bar nearly disappears. Three distinct winner groups exist: (1) insider traders with non-public information, (2) skilled winners (~3% of users, ~20% of profits) who trade broadly, react fast, and avoid behavioral biases, and (3) market makers (0.1% of accounts) who provide liquidity and extract spread from the losing majority. The "wisdom of crowds" that makes prediction markets accurate is actually produced almost entirely by group 2 — the remaining majority doesn't produce accuracy, it funds it.

🏆 The Three Groups Who Actually Make Money

Everyone else is funding their wins. Click each group to expand.

👲 Group 1: Insider Traders
Illegal-ish Non-public info $143M+ on Polymarket
People with advance knowledge of events bet against those who don't. $50K on Taylor Swift's engagement. $400K+ on Maduro's capture. $1M+ on Google's search terms. Researchers found $143M in suspicious trades over 2 years.

How it works

  • Someone knows an event is about to happen (or has already happened but isn't public yet)
  • They place large bets on low-probability outcomes that are about to resolve
  • Example: an account made its first trade on "Maduro capture" just days before the event, placed $40K on contracts under 10¢, and stopped trading immediately after
  • Three major bets were placed after the capture happened but before the information went public

The research

  • Joshua & Moran Ofir analyzed 2 years of Polymarket activity for unusual behavior
  • Suspicious accounts made $143M between Feb 2024 and Feb 2026
  • That sounds like a lot, but it's less than 1% of Polymarket's total volume in that period
  • Insider trading is a massive issue but it's only one piece of the puzzle

Can you detect it?

  • Accounts that appear right before an event and vanish after
  • Large bets on very low-priced contracts (under 10¢)
  • Trades placed in the window between an event happening and it becoming public
  • This is where social intelligence and Hermes monitoring comes in — see the INSIDER TRACKING tab
🧠 Group 2: Skilled Winners (The 3%)
3% of users 20% of profits The "smart money"
A tiny group of skilled traders who trade broadly across many markets, react quickly to news, anticipate changes faster than others, and — crucially — avoid the behavioral biases that trap everyone else.

What they do better

  • Trade broadly — across many kinds of markets, not just one niche
  • React quickly to news — they're first to adjust positions when new information drops
  • Anticipate future changes — they see what's coming before the crowd
  • Avoid the "optimism tax" — they don't fall for the traps that catch average users

The "Optimism Tax" — what skilled winners avoid

  • Over-betting on YES: YES is green and listed first. Design cues nudge people toward clicking it. Average users lose money this way.
  • Overestimating low probabilities: Cheap long-shot bets (5¢, 10¢) feel like bargains but generally lose money. They're overpriced — a 10¢ bet wins less than 10% of the time.
  • Ignoring expensive bets: A 90¢ bet wins more than 90% of the time (it's underpriced). But people avoid them because the profit per trade is small.

The key insight

  • Skilled winners are defined as much by what they don't do as what they do
  • They don't chase long shots. They don't over-bet YES. They don't let design cues influence their decisions.
  • They treat it like a math problem, not a feeling
🏦 Group 3: Market Makers (The 0.1%)
0.1% of accounts Institutional Billions deployed
Big institutional trading firms pump billions into prediction markets so that when you want to place a bet, there's always someone on the other side. They're not betting — they're providing liquidity and extracting profit from every trade.

What market makers do

  • They provide the money that makes markets function — without them, there's no one to bet against
  • They take the other side of your trade and profit from the spread
  • Kalshi and Polymarket's business models depend on market makers
  • Market makers' profit comes directly from the losing majority of users

The paradox

  • Prediction markets say "We aren't the house" and "We don't win when you lose" — and that's technically true
  • But the house (market makers) does win when you lose
  • And the platforms need market makers to exist
  • So prediction markets are fundamentally dependent on users losing money

The researchers' conclusion

  • "Markets often fail because they don't have enough dumb money as opposed to not having enough smart money"

Prediction markets are right because smart people make them right. Not because of everybody. The crowd isn't wise — the smart few are wise, and everybody else pays for it.

The "wisdom of crowds" idea behind prediction markets isn't quite right. Research shows that prediction market accuracy is created by skilled winners (the 3%), not by the crowd. The majority of participants don't produce accuracy — they fund it. Smart money is what makes prediction markets accurate, and smart money only uses prediction markets because it can reliably feed off dumb money.

Smart money is what makes prediction markets accurate, and smart money only uses prediction markets because it can reliably feed off dumb money — as long as new dumb money keeps coming in.

Prediction markets DO generate accurate predictions. And they DO make some people money. But the average user is neither making money nor helping generate accuracy. #!/usr/bin/env python3
"""Social intelligence monitor for prediction markets.
Watches key accounts and flags market-relevant posts."""

import subprocess, json, os, sqlite3, hashlib
from datetime import datetime

# Accounts to monitor: (username, category, relevance_keywords)
SOURCES = [
    # Fed & Economics
    ("fedchair", "fed", ["rate", "inflation", "employment", "policy"]),
    ("laborsec", "labor", ["jobs", "unemployment", "wages"]),
    # Politics
    ("speakerpelosi", "congress", ["vote", "bill", "shutdown", "debt"]),
    ("senategovt", "congress", ["confirm", "nominee", "hearing"]),
    # Geopolitics
    ("natsecadvisor", "security", ["sanctions", "military", "deal", "talks"]),
    # Crypto
   ("cz_binance", "crypto", ["regulation", "exchange", "token", "defi"]),
    ("saylor", "crypto", ["bitcoin", "btc", "sec", "etf"]),
]

DB_PATH = os.path.expanduser("~/kalshi-bot/social_intelligence.db")
KALSHI_API = "https://external-api.kalshi.com/trade-api/v2/markets?status=open&limit=200"

def init_db():
    conn = sqlite3.connect(DB_PATH)
    conn.execute("""CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS posts (
        id TEXT PRIMARY KEY,
        username TEXT,
        content TEXT,
        posted_at TEXT,
        category TEXT,
        signal_strength REAL,
        markets TEXT,
        processed_at TEXT
    )""")
    conn.execute("""CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS signals (
        id INTEGER PRIMARY KEY AUTOINCREMENT,
        post_id TEXT,
        market_ticker TEXT,
        signal_type TEXT,
        confidence REAL,
        action TEXT,
        created_at TEXT
    )""")
    conn.commit()
    return conn

def fetch_latest_tweets(username, limit=10):
    """Use xurl to fetch recent tweets from a user."""
    try:
        result = subprocess.run(
            ["xurl", "user", "tweets", "--user", username, "--limit", str(limit)],
            capture_output=True, text=True, timeout=30
        )
        if result.returncode == 0:
            return json.loads(result.stdout)
    except Exception as e:
        print(f"Error fetching @{username}: {e}")
    return []

def analyze_post(post_text, keywords):
    """Simple keyword-based signal detection. Replace with Hermes LLM for production."""
    text_lower = post_text.lower()
    matches = [kw for kw in keywords if kw in text_lower]
    if not matches:
        return 0.0, []
    # Signal strength based on keyword matches and urgency words
    urgency = ["breaking", "urgent", "just", "now", "announced", "confirmed", "exclusive"]
    urgency_score = sum(1 for u in urgency if u in text_lower) * 0.2
    strength = min(1.0, len(matches) * 0.3 + urgency_score)
    return strength, matches

def map_to_markets(category, keywords):
    """Map a signal category to relevant Kalshi market tickers."""
    # This is a simplified mapping — expand based on current active markets
    MAPPING = {
        "fed": ["KXRATE", "KXFED", "KXINFLATION", "KXCPI"],
        "labor": ["KXUNEMP", "KXJOBS", "KXNONFARM"],
        "congress": ["KXSHUTDOWN", "KXDEBTCEIL", "KXVOTE"],
        "security": ["KXSANCTIONS", "KXTRADE", "KXWAR"],
        "crypto": ["KXBTC", "KXETH", "KXCRYPTO", "KXSEC"],
    }
    return MAPPING.get(category, [])

def run():
    conn = conn = init_db()
    total_signals = 0

    for username, category, keywords in SOURCES:
        tweets = fetch_latest_tweets(username)
        for tweet in tweets:
            tweet_id = tweet.get("id") or hashlib.md5(
                tweet.get("text", "").encode()
            ).hexdigest()

            # Skip if already processed
            existing = conn.execute(
                "SELECT 1 FROM posts WHERE id=?", (tweet_id,)
            ).fetchone()
            if existing:
                continue

            content = tweet.get("text", "")
            strength, matched = analyze_post(content, keywords)
            markets = map_to_markets(category, matched)

            conn.execute(
                "INSERT OR IGNORE INTO posts VALUES (?,?,?,?,?,?,?,?)",
                (tweet_id, username, content, tweet.get("created_at", ""),
                 category, strength, json.dumps(markets), datetime.now().isoformat())
            )

            if strength > 0.3 and markets:
                for ticker in markets:
                    conn.execute(
                        "INSERT INTO signals (post_id, market_ticker, signal_type, confidence, action, created_at) VALUES (?,?,?,?,?,?)",
                        (tweet_id, ticker, category, strength, "WATCH", datetime.now().isoformat())
                    )
                    total_signals += 1
                print(f"🚨 SIGNAL: @{username} → {markets} (strength: {strength:.1f})")
                print(f"   Post: {content[:120]}...")

    conn.commit()
    conn.close()
    print(f"\n[{datetime.now().strftime('%H:%M')}] Scan complete. {total_signals} new signals.")
    return total_signals

if __name__ == "__main__":
    run()

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""Execute trades on Kalshi based on social signals."""

import requests, json, os, base64
from cryptography.hazmat.primitives import hashes, serialization
from cryptography.hazmat.primitives.asymmetric import padding

KALSHI_API = "https://external-api.kalshi.com/trade-api/v2"
API_KEY = os.environ["KALSHI_API_KEY"]
PRIVATE_KEY_PATH = os.path.expanduser("~/.kalshi/private_key.pem")

def load_private_key():
    with open(PRIVATE_KEY_PATH, "rb") as f:
        return serialization.load_pem_private_key(f.read(), password=None)

def sign_request(method, path, body=""):
    key = load_private_key()
    timestamp = str(int(time.time() * 1000))
    message = f"{timestamp}{method}{path}{body}"
    signature = key.sign(message.encode(), padding.PKCS1v15(), hashes.SHA256())
    return base64.b64encode(signature).decode(), timestamp

def place_order(ticker, side, count, price_cents):
    """Place a limit order on Kalshi."""
    path = "/v2/portfolio/orders"
    body = json.dumps({
        "ticker": ticker,
        "side": side,  # "yes" or "no"
        "count": count,
        "type": "limit",
        "price": price_cents
    })
    sig, ts = sign_request("POST", path, body)
    headers = {
        "KALSHI-ACCESS-KEY": API_KEY,
        "KALSHI-ACCESS-SIGNATURE": sig,
        "KALSHI-ACCESS-TIMESTAMP": ts,
        "Content-Type": "application/json"
    }
    r = requests.post(f"{KALSHI_API}{path}", headers=headers, data=body)
    return r.json()

def get_market_price(ticker):
    """Get current market price for a ticker."""
    r = requests.get(f"{KALSHI_API}/markets/{ticker}", timeout=10)
    data = r.json().get("market", {})
    return data
